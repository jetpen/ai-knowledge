# How to Become AI Native

## Article plain_text

Most people think AI native means using ChatGPT at work.
The real thing goes much further.
On The Startup Ideas Podcast, Greg Eisenberg sat down with his co-founder Theo from LCA for a sub-60-minute masterclass. LCA builds AI products for some of the biggest companies on the planet. Theo shared the system they usually keep in-house.
Here's the whole thing.
The definition
 
An AI native org runs on three rules:
People manage agents.
Agents read and write to the company.
The company gets smarter over time.
That's it. Everything below sits on top of those three.
The payoff is speed. You produce work in minutes. You hear from customers fast. That speed becomes a moat.
The stack: People → Agents → Context
 
People come first.
An AI native org starts with AI native people. You can buy every tool and every agent. If your people struggle to manage them, the whole thing stalls.
The reframe: everyone is a manager now.
Before AI, most of your time went to the middle of the work. The execution. The drafting. AI eats the middle. You get freed to focus on the bookends: deciding what to do, and judging whether it's good.
Andy Grove's line fits here. A manager is judged by the output of their team. Your team is now agents. Treat them like new hires.
Agents are the second layer.
An agent is a model using tools in a loop. (Barry Zhang of Anthropic.) Give it an environment. Hand it tools. Set its goals.
Three levels of agent use:
You chat with ChatGPT or Claude.
Agents run, but you babysit. Approve, approve, approve.
Agent autonomy. It runs for days like a trusted new hire.
To reach level 3, an agent needs four things:
 
A clear goal.
The skills.
The tools.
The context.
Picture your first day at a new job. Build a board deck by next week, with no manager and no background. You'd fail. So does an agent missing any of the four.
Two ideas make agents reliable.
Skills are markdown files. Playbooks the agent reads before it acts.
Skill chains run those playbooks back to back. Skill 1 fires, calls skill 2, then skill 3. Chains are how you fight hallucination. Agents love to fake it till they make it. A QA skill at the end catches it.
Evals give you eyes on the output. What did the agent do, how did it get there, and is it an 8, a 9, or a 10 against the standard you set.
Context is the third layer. The foundation.
Theo calls it the brain. It's folders of markdown files, organized so agents can search, read, and write back.
The loop:
 
Capture → Curate → Store → Execute → Experience → back into the system.
Capture: a routine runs every hour and pulls from Slack, meetings, emails, Linear. (A scheduled task on steroids.)
Curate: a librarian step. Read it, clean it, file it, decide what to skip, flag the triggers.
Store: the brain. Folders, files, and READMEs that guide the agent to the right place.
Execute: agents pull context and do the work.
Experience: customers feel it, and their reactions flow back in.
One detail people miss: traces. The cutting-room-floor decisions. Why you chose A over B. Most teams bury that in a graveyard of files. The brain reads it and turns it into lessons.
Proof: two workflows
 
Workflow one: the proposal.
A client asks for a proposal in a meeting or an email. The brain catches the trigger and fires a skill chain of three skills: build a proposal microsite, write the copy in Theo's voice, then QA it so nothing is over-promised or made up. It deploys live on a link and pings him in Slack.
Time: under 5 minutes.
It also bakes in personal moments from past calls. A client once told him a record store clerk hands you a record and says "trust me." That exact line shows up in the proposal. Speed to signal closes deals. This approach has driven millions in revenue for LCA closing Fortune 2000s.
Workflow two: the prototype.
Theo speaks a feature idea out loud for a Spotify-style app: a daily playlist of three handpicked songs on the homepage, with reasons why, plus save, share, and play. The goal is to build it in under 10 minutes, match the design system, and make it testable.
A skill chain builds it. A second chain runs a usability test, synthesizes the feedback, and drafts a V2 in the same session.
A functional prototype with real feedback used to take 1 to 2 weeks. Here it took minutes.
Light on context? Bootstrap it
You can start with far less than LCA has.
Go to Mobbin. It has an MCP now and a library of real app flows. Grab a design system. Wrap a skill around it. Plug in the MCP. Pick your output, find the MCP that feeds it, and load context over time.
The startup idea
 
Here's the service business hiding in all of this. Sell the system itself. A 30-day sprint or an AI acceleration team.
Niche down on three vectors:
Industry (restaurants are hot, fragmented, and ready).
Function (which team you serve).
Company size (big enough to carry a budget).
Then use a simple 2x2: niche to general, low frequency to high frequency.
Start with niche and high frequency. Those are layups you can show on a sales call. Move to general next. Save the high-value, low-frequency work for last, where the ROI runs highest.
The close
The whole thing sounds like it needs a technical genius. It needs a manager.
Think through what your agents need to win: a goal, skills, tools, context. Give it to them. Then get out of the way.
Scrape your knee. Ship something today.
